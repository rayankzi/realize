# Claude Prompts: Bad vs Good vs Great — The 9-Element Framework

**Topic/Category:** AI Prompting / Productivity
**Primary Goal of the Post:** Teach users how to dramatically improve their Claude AI prompts by progressing from vague, generic prompts to structured, context-rich prompts using a 9-element framework.

---

## Executive Summary

Most people only get 20% of Claude's capabilities because their prompts lack structure and context. This carousel walks through six real-world business scenarios — LinkedIn posts, startup validation, marketing strategy, competitor analysis, SaaS ideation, and AI automation — showing the stark difference between bad, good, and great prompts. The core principle: a great prompt tells Claude what you need, how to think through it, and exactly when to stop, using a repeatable 9-element framework.

## Key Insights & Main Ideas

- **Context is everything:** Bad prompts fail because they give Claude zero context, resulting in generic, surface-level answers that offer nothing new.
- **Specificity scales quality:** Moving from "Is this startup idea good?" to a structured prompt with role, audience, constraints, and tone produces dramatically better output.
- **The 9-element framework:** Every great prompt is built around: Task context, Tone context, Background data/documents/images, Detailed task description and rules, Examples, Conversation history, Immediate task/request, Think step by step, and Output formatting.
- **"Previous context" is the secret weapon:** Nearly every great prompt example includes a "Previous context" section that tells Claude what NOT to do — e.g., "I do not want a generic full-funnel plan" or "I want a realistic answer, not encouragement."
- **"Think step by step" closes every great prompt:** This simple instruction consistently appears at the end, encouraging Claude to reason through the problem methodically.

  - Bad: "Is this startup idea good?"

  - Great: Provides structured placeholders ([INSERT IDEA], [CUSTOMER TYPE], [MODEL]), asks for risks and strongest signals, and explicitly requests a realistic answer — not encouragement.
- **Slide 4 (Marketing Strategy):**
  - Bad: "Give me a marketing plan."
  - Good: "Create a marketing strategy for a startup selling [PRODUCT]."
  - Great: Includes product, target audience, budget placeholders, focuses on highest-leverage channels, rejects generic full-funnel plans, and demands practical actions implementable with limited time and budget.
- **Slide 5 (Competitor Analysis):**
  - Bad: "Analyse my competitors."

  - Great: Specifies company and competitor names, requests positioning/pricing/marketing analysis, asks to identify where competitors are genuinely stronger, and looks for market openings.

## Verbatim Templates & Scripts

> **LinkedIn Post — Great Prompt:**
> You are a founder writing on LinkedIn about startup strategy.
> Your audience is early-stage founders building their first companies.
> Explain why niching down early accelerates growth.
> Use short paragraphs and practical advice.
> Hooks could include:
> Most founders delay this decision for years.
> Your niche determines your growth speed.
> Previous context: I write practical content for founders and want this post to sound direct, useful, and experience-led rather than motivational.
> Write a LinkedIn post under 2900 characters.
> Think step by step.

> **Business Idea Validation — Great Prompt:**
> You are a founder evaluating a potential startup idea.
> Create a short analysis with clear risks and signals based on the context below.
> Business idea: [INSERT IDEA]
> Target customer: [CUSTOMER TYPE]
> Revenue model: [MODEL]
> Evaluate whether this idea has real market demand.
> Identify the biggest risks and strongest signals it could succeed.
> Previous context: I care more about commercial viability than whether the idea is interesting. I want a realistic answer, not encouragement.
> Write the evaluation.
> Think step by step.

> **Marketing Strategy — Great Prompt:**
> You are a founder building a marketing strategy for your startup.
> Product: [PRODUCT]
> Target audience: [AUDIENCE]
> Budget: [BUDGET]
> Design a strategy to acquire the first 1000 customers.
> Focus on the highest leverage channels.
> Previous context: I do not want a generic full-funnel plan. I want the most practical actions I could implement first with limited time and budget.
> Write the plan.
> Think step by step.

> **Competitor Analysis — Great Prompt:**
> You are a founder analysing competitors in your market.
> Company: [YOUR COMPANY]
> Competitors:
> [COMPETITOR 1]
> [COMPETITOR 2]
> Analyse their positioning, pricing and marketing.
> Identify their biggest advantage and biggest weakness.
> Previous context: I am not looking for surface-level observations. I want to understand where they are genuinely stronger than us and where there is an opening in the market.
> Write the analysis.
> Think step by step.

> **SaaS Idea Generation — Great Prompt:**
> You are a founder exploring profitable SaaS opportunities.
> Industry: marketing
> Customers: agencies and creators
> Generate SaaS ideas that solve real problems for this audience.
> Prioritise ideas with strong demand and high margins.
> Previous context: I care more about commercially viable ideas than clever ideas. I want concepts people would actually pay for, not novelty tools.
> Think step by step.

> **AI Automation — Great Prompt:**
> You are a founder looking to automate parts of your business using AI.
> Business type: [BUSINESS TYPE]
> Team size: [TEAM SIZE]
> Identify tasks that could be automated with AI.
> Focus on repetitive tasks that consume team time.
> Suggest automations that could realistically be implemented within 30 days.
> Previous context: I do not want futuristic ideas or enterprise-grade projects. I want practical automations that save time quickly for a small team.
> Write the recommendations.
> Think step by step.
