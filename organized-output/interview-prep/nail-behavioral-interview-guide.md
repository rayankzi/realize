## Executive Summary

This carousel post by a self-described "10x intern" and Cornell Law student breaks down the behavioral interview into actionable components. Rather than generic advice, it provides specific frameworks for answering problem-solving and pressure questions, a strategic approach to discussing strengths and weaknesses, and a library of ready-to-use STAR-method transition phrases. The core message: behavioral interviews are a learnable skill, not an innate talent, and with the right preparation you can achieve a near-100% interview-to-offer conversion rate.

## Key Insights & Main Ideas

- **Problem-solving questions are about process, not just results:** Interviewers want to see how you broke down the problem, who you involved, and what your approach was — not just the outcome.
- **Strengths should be role-relevant and evidence-backed:** Pick 2-3 strengths that directly tie to the job, show don't tell with micro-examples, and connect each to team value.
- **Weaknesses must be genuine and paired with a growth pivot:** Avoid cliches like "I work too hard." Choose a real growth area that isn't core to the role, own it, then spend more time on what you've done to address it.
- **The exact words you use matter:** Specific STAR-method transition phrases signal confidence and clarity, helping you move smoothly between Situation, Task, Action, and Result.
- **Preparation beats improvisation:** Having 6-8 flexible stories ready, using specific numbers, and ending answers cleanly are hallmarks of a well-prepared candidate.

**Slide 1 — Cover/Hook:** A mirror selfie with bold text: "How to nail the behavioral interview" with the subtext "(when you have no idea what to say)." Establishes relatability and draws in the target audience of anxious interviewees.

**Slide 2 — Problem-Solving & Pressure Questions:** Covers three common behavioral prompts:
1. *"Tell me about a time you solved a challenging problem."* — Focus on process over result. Use a 15% setup, 65% thought process, 20% outcome split. Show reasoning ("I considered X and Y, chose X because...") and include collaboration.
2. *"Give me an example of thinking on your feet."* — Pick a moment where something unexpected happened. Walk through real-time evaluation: what did you assess, prioritize, or rule out? Emphasize composure, not just brilliance.
3. *"How do you handle multiple responsibilities at once?"* — Be specific about your actual systems (prioritization frameworks, time-blocking). Acknowledge that real challenges happen, not just "I stay calm and make lists."

**Slide 3 — Strengths & Weaknesses (Self-Assessment):**
- **Strengths:** Pick role-relevant ones (2-3), show don't tell with micro-examples, and connect to team value by explaining why you'd be a fit for *this specific team*.
- **Weaknesses:** Choose something real (not "I work too hard"), own it then pivot to what you've done to improve, and end with a specific habit you do differently now — not a vague "I'm working on it."
- **What they're really assessing:** Self-awareness and coachability. Interviewers aren't expecting perfection — they want to see you understand yourself clearly and take accountability honestly.

## Verbatim Templates & Scripts

### STAR Transition Phrases

**Setting the Scene (Situation + Task):**
> "In my role as..., I was responsible for..."
> "I was specifically tasked with..."
> "The challenge we were up against was..."

**Walking Through Your Actions:**
> "I started by assessing..."
> "I made the decision to... because..."
> "Rather than waiting for direction, I..."
> "I weighed the options and determined..."

**Landing the Result:**
> "As a result, we were able to..."
> "The outcome was [specific metric]..."
> "I've carried that approach into everything since."

### Do This / Avoid This Checklist

**Do This:**
> - Use "I" instead of "we" — own your contributions
> - Include specific numbers wherever possible
> - Pause briefly before answering to gather thoughts
> - Prepare 6-8 stories that flex to multiple questions
> - End every answer cleanly — don't trail off

**Avoid This:**
> - "We did everything together" — avoids accountability
> - Stories with no clear outcome or resolution
> - Answers longer than 2.5 min without a pause
> - Generic answers that fit any company
> - Criticizing a past employer or teammate
