# Networking Feels Cringe Because You Only Do It When You're Desperate

**Topic/Category:** Career Development, Networking
**Primary Goal of the Post:** To reframe how people approach professional networking by exposing the common mistakes that make it feel awkward, transactional, and ineffective — and to encourage building relationships proactively rather than reactively.

---

## Executive Summary

This carousel breaks down why networking feels uncomfortable for most people: they only do it when they need something. The creator, who moved to Dubai four years ago with no network, shares the key behaviors she had to unlearn — from lazy outreach messages to treating connections as transactions. The core message is that networking becomes natural and effective when you invest in relationships *before* you need them, not after. The caption reinforces this by offering a "TRACKER" system to help followers build consistent networking habits.

## Key Insights & Main Ideas

- **Insight 1:** Networking feels cringe because most people only reach out when they're desperate — for a job, referral, or introduction — and that desperation is transparent to the recipient.
- **Insight 2:** Generic, lazy questions like "What advice would you give someone starting out?" signal that you haven't invested any thought into the person you're reaching out to.
- **Insight 3:** Most LinkedIn outreach reads like a cold call with better formatting — messages like "I came across your profile and was really impressed" or "I'd love to pick your brain" are entirely self-serving and get ignored.
- **Insight 4:** People fail to act on the impulse to connect in the moment, and by the time they finally reach out months later, it's because they need something.
- **Insight 5:** Networks don't disappear overnight — they go cold because people lose track and don't follow up after good conversations.
- **Insight 6:** Treating connection as a transaction (see someone interesting → want something → message them, in that order) skips the crucial step of letting the other person notice you first.
