# 5 ML Projects That Actually Get You Interviews (Not Titanic & MNIST)

**Topic/Category:** Machine Learning / Data Science Career Development
**Primary Goal of the Post:** To convince ML/DS students and job seekers to replace basic toy projects (Titanic, MNIST, Iris) with production-grade projects that demonstrate real-world engineering skills to hiring managers.

---

## Executive Summary

The creator (@sujar.tech) shares a personal story of struggling to land internship interviews despite having ML projects on their resume. The root cause wasn't their resume formatting or keywords — it was that their projects were generic toy datasets every recruiter has seen thousands of times. After replacing them with production-oriented projects that demonstrate full-lifecycle ML engineering, they started receiving interview invitations and compliments on their portfolio. The carousel presents 5 specific project categories that signal real engineering capability to hiring managers.

## Key Insights & Main Ideas

- **Insight 1:** Toy projects (Titanic, Iris, MNIST classifiers) are fine for learning but actively hurt your job applications because hiring managers have seen them thousands of times and they don't prove you can build real systems.
- **Insight 2:** The gap between "I can call model.fit()" and "I can build and deploy an ML system" is what separates candidates who get interviews from those who don't.
- **Insight 3:** Production-grade skills — pipelines, deployment, monitoring, system design, CI/CD — are what senior engineers and hiring managers actually look for and ask about in interviews.
- **Insight 4:** You don't need novel research to stand out; taking existing work (like a Kaggle solution) and shipping it as a real product with proper engineering practices is highly impressive.

## Detailed Breakdown / Step-by-Step

- **Slide 1 (Hook):** "Built 6 ML projects. Got 0 interviews. Here's What I Changed." — The creator sits frustrated, surrounded by labels like "ML Pipelines" and "Neural Networks," establishing the relatable pain point.

- **Slide 2 (The Problem):** Toy projects don't impress hiring managers. They've seen 1,000 Titanic datasets and MNIST classifiers. You need projects that prove you can build real systems.

- **Slide 3 (Project 01 — End-to-End ML Pipeline):** Build a full pipeline: data ingestion → feature engineering → model training → evaluation → deployment. This shows you understand the entire lifecycle, not just `model.fit()`.

- **Slide 4 (Project 02 — Fine-Tune Models):** Fine-tune an open-source LLM on domain-specific data. Use LoRA or QLoRA with real messy data — not a clean HuggingFace dataset. Key skills demonstrated: tokenization, evaluation, and cost trade-offs.

- **Slide 5 (Project 03 — Real-Time Inference API):** Deploy a model behind a FastAPI endpoint with monitoring, logging, and auto-scaling. Bonus: add latency tracking and A/B testing. This is what production ML actually looks like.

- **Slide 6 (Project 04 — System Design):** Document a full ML system design: trade-offs, architecture decisions, scaling strategy, and failure modes. This is what senior engineers actually get asked in interviews.

- **Slide 7 (Project 05 — Kaggle → Production):** Take a Kaggle competition solution and ship it as a real product. Add data validation, CI/CD, monitoring, and a user-facing interface. Bridge the gap between notebook and production.
