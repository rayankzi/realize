# Remove These AI Fingerprints From Your Cover Letter & Resume

**Topic/Category:** Career / Resume Writing
**Primary Goal of the Post:** To warn job seekers about the telltale signs that AI-generated resume and cover letter content leaves behind, and how to edit it so it sounds human and polished.

---

## Executive Summary

AI tools like ChatGPT are increasingly used to draft resumes and cover letters, but they leave behind recognizable patterns that recruiters and hiring managers can spot instantly. This post identifies six common "AI fingerprints" — from buzzword clusters to repetitive bullet structures — and urges job seekers to carefully edit AI-generated content so it reads naturally and stands out rather than blending into the flood of robotic applications.

## Key Insights & Main Ideas

- **Buzzword Clusters:** AI tends to pack dense corporate jargon into single sentences or phrases (e.g., "I am a strategic cross-functional data-driven decision maker"), which sounds unnatural and over-the-top.
- **3 Things Per Sentence:** AI defaults to the "rule of 3," listing three items in a row (e.g., "I bring strong leadership, communication, and problem-solving skills"), creating a predictable cadence.
- **Overuse of Professional-Toned Words:** Words like "leveraging," "orchestrated," "pioneered," "championed," "comprehensive," and "strengthened" are AI favorites and appear far too frequently.
- **General Statements Without Measurable Detail:** AI will always claim impact without specifics (e.g., "I've led many initiatives that improved operational efficiency across teams") — lacking the numbers and context that make claims credible.
- **Em Dash Overuse:** Em dashes add rhythm and a polished feel to writing. AI automatically inserts them everywhere, making them a dead giveaway.
- **Bullet Formula Repetition:** Every AI-generated bullet point tends to follow the exact same sentence structure, making the entire resume feel robotic and templated. Variety is essential.
