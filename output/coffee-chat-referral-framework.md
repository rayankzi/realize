# The Coffee Chat Framework That Actually Gets You Referrals

**Topic/Category:** Career Advice, Networking, Job Search
**Primary Goal of the Reel:** Teach job seekers how to structure networking coffee chats strategically so they lead to referrals, not just pleasant conversations.

---

## Executive Summary

This reel breaks down why most people fail at coffee chats and provides a four-step framework to turn casual networking conversations into genuine referral opportunities. The creator argues that coffee chats aren't friendly conversations — they're trust-building exercises where you need to show alignment with a role and make it easy for the other person to advocate for you. The content includes specific scripts for each stage of the conversation, from opening to closing with a direct ask.

## Core Insights & Takeaways

- **Insight 1:** Most coffee chats fail because people ask surface-level questions, keep things too vague, talk about their background without connecting it to the role, and end with no clear next step.
- **Insight 2:** A coffee chat is fundamentally a trust-building opportunity — the goal is to make it easy for someone to back you, not just to have a nice conversation.
- **Insight 3:** Referrals don't come from directly asking for them. They come from demonstrating alignment and competence throughout the conversation so the other person naturally wants to advocate for you.
- **Insight 4:** Positioning yourself mid-conversation (connecting your experience to what they describe) is more effective than a standalone pitch about your background.

## Narrative & Step-by-Step Breakdown

**Beginning — The Problem:**
The creator opens by calling out a common pain point: getting on a coffee chat and not knowing what to say. They list four common mistakes people make.

**Middle — The Four-Step Framework:**

1. **Open the conversation properly** — Set the tone by thanking them, expressing interest in their journey, and framing the call around your own career exploration.
2. **Ask better questions** — Go beyond generic questions. Ask about what skills matter on the team, what projects are prioritized, and what makes someone stand out.
3. **Position yourself as you go** — Instead of giving a monologue about your background, weave your experience into the conversation naturally by connecting it to what they share.
4. **Close properly** — Ask directly whether your background could be a good fit. If they say yes, that's the opening to a referral.

**End — The Principle:**
Referrals come from making it easy for someone to trust and advocate for you — not from asking outright.

## Verbatim Templates & Scripts

### Step 1: Opening Script

> "Thanks so much for taking the time - I'd love to hear a bit more about your journey into [team/role] and ask a few questions because I'm exploring a similar move myself."

### Step 2: Better Questions to Ask

> "What does someone on this team need to be really good at?"

> "What kinds of projects are most important in your role right now?"

> "What tends to make someone stand out in this team?"

### Step 3: Positioning Lines

> "That's really interesting - it actually reminds me of a project I worked on where I…"

> "A big part of my background has been around…"

> "That's the kind of work I've been trying to move closer to as well."

### Step 4: Closing Question

> "Based on our conversation, do you think my background could be a good fit for a team like this?"
