**Topic/Category:** Career / Internships

## Executive Summary

This carousel post from a 10x intern, Cornell Law student, and Forbes 30 Under 30 early career educator shares a complete cover letter template designed specifically for internship applications. Unlike generic cover letter advice, the post provides a full letter template with bracketed placeholders, then dedicates two slides to a detailed "how to fill in your letter" guide with concrete examples for every type of student background — from research and engineering to part-time jobs and volunteer work. The core philosophy: show what you *did*, what you *learned*, and why *this specific company* matters to you.

## Key Insights & Main Ideas

- **Lead with company-specific mission alignment:** The opening paragraph should reference a real initiative, product, or mission element of the company — not generic praise. Spend 10 minutes researching their website, news, or LinkedIn before writing this line.
- **Quantify your most impressive achievement:** Whether it's an internship, research role, class project, or campus leadership position, lead with a specific, quantified accomplishment that demonstrates concrete impact.
- **Name concrete skills, not soft skills:** Instead of saying "I'm a hard worker," describe what you learned to *do* — e.g., "move fast without sacrificing rigor" or "translate ambiguous problems into structured, actionable plans."
- **Show range with a second experience:** The second paragraph should highlight a different dimension of your skill set to demonstrate versatility and adaptability.
- **Close with genuine, specific alignment:** Generic enthusiasm gets screened out. Reference a specific product decision, published value, recent initiative, or stated value you can trace to real company behavior.
- **Your experience counts more than you think:** A part-time job demonstrates composure and judgment, a class project shows initiative and execution, a personal project shows ownership. The goal is to show you did it seriously and learned something real.

**Slide 2: The Full Cover Letter Template**
A complete, formatted cover letter with the following structure:
1. Header with name, phone, city/state, school email, and LinkedIn
2. Opening paragraph: Company-specific mission alignment + position you're applying for + your background/field
3. First body paragraph: Most impressive experience + quantified achievement + concrete skill demonstrated
4. Second body paragraph: Second relevant experience showing a different skill set + connecting both to fluency across domains
5. Closing paragraph: One genuinely specific detail about the company + authentic alignment between your goals and theirs
6. Professional sign-off

**Slide 3: How to Fill In Your Letter (Part 1)**
Detailed guidance on filling in each bracketed placeholder:
- **[Specific Initiative, Product, or Mission Element]** — Must be real and specific, not generic. Examples provided.
- **[Field/Area]** — Your major, concentration, or area of most relevant experience.
- **[Most Impressive Experience] + [Quantified Achievement]** — Examples by background type (internship, research, academic project, campus leadership, early-in-college).
- **[Concrete Skill]** — Name what you learned to *do*, not a soft skill label.

**Slide 4: How to Fill In Your Letter (Part 2)**
Continued guidance:
- **[Second Experience] + [Second Achievement]** — Examples by background type (part-time/service job, volunteer/community work, relevant coursework, personal/freelance project).
- **[One Genuinely Specific Detail] + [Authentic Alignment]** — What separates serious applicants from everyone else. Find one specific, traceable thing about the company.
- **Final motivational note:** Students often disqualify themselves before a recruiter ever sees their application. The goal is never to have the most prestigious background — it's to show that whatever you've done, you did it seriously and learned something real.

## Verbatim Templates & Scripts

**Complete Cover Letter Template:**

> **Firstname Lastname**
> (123) 456 7891 · City, State · School Email · LinkedIn (hyperlink your own)
> ___
>
> Dear [Company Name] Recruiting Team,
>
> [Company Name]'s work on [Specific Initiative, Product, or Mission Element — e.g., "expanding access to affordable financial tools for underserved communities" or "building privacy-first AI infrastructure"] is exactly the kind of problem I want to spend my career on. I am applying for the [Position Title] internship because I believe my background in [Field/Area] has prepared me to contribute meaningfully: not just to learn, but to deliver.
>
> At [Most Impressive Organization or University Role], I [Specific, Quantified Achievement — e.g., "led a 6-person team that built and launched a machine learning model reducing customer churn prediction error by 23%" or "drafted federal policy briefs adopted by two sitting members of Congress"]. That experience taught me how to [Concrete Skill Demonstrated, e.g., "move fast without sacrificing rigor" or "translate ambiguous problems into structured, actionable plans"] — something I know matters in a role like this one.
>
> Before that, at [Second Relevant Experience], I [Second Achievement — different skill set from above, showing range]. Together, these experiences have given me fluency in [Skill/Domain #1] and [Skill/Domain #2], and more importantly, the judgment to know when to apply each.
>
> What draws me specifically to [Company Name] is [One Genuinely Specific Detail — a product decision, a published value, a recent initiative — that shows you've done your homework]. I'm not looking for any internship; I'm looking for this one, because [1–2 sentences articulating authentic alignment between your goals and theirs].
>
> Thank you for your consideration.
>
> Sincerely,
>
> Firstname Lastname

**Concrete Skill Examples (use instead of soft skills):**

> - "move fast without sacrificing rigor"
> - "translate ambiguous problems into structured, actionable plans"
> - "communicate technical findings clearly to non-technical stakeholders"
> - "hold myself accountable to outcomes, not just effort"
> - "build systems that don't depend on my constant presence"

**Company Alignment Examples:**

> - "What draws me specifically to [Company] is your decision to [specific product or policy choice]. Most companies in this space have taken the easier path, and the fact that your team didn't tell me something important about how decisions get made here."
> - "I've been thinking about the problem of [X] since [genuine reason], and the approach [Company] is taking is the most rigorous one I've encountered."
