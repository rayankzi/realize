# How a Sophomore From a Non-Target School Landed a Microsoft Internship

**Topic/Category:** Career / Tech Internships / Computer Science
**Primary Goal of the Post:** To share a step-by-step blueprint for landing a Microsoft software engineering internship without the typical advantages of a top-20 school, referrals, or prior projects.

---

## Executive Summary
Ella Gonzales, a sophomore CS major from a non-top-20 university, secured a Microsoft internship by combining early applications, strategic networking, a polished resume, campus involvement, and thorough interview preparation. Her carousel breaks down each phase of her process — from the moment applications open to the final interview — proving that intentional preparation and resourcefulness can outweigh pedigree. The post culminates with a lead-magnet offer: a comprehensive "PRIME Big Tech Interview Prep Guide" document she compiled with all the resources she used.

## Key Insights & Main Ideas
* **Insight 1: Speed matters more than perfection.** She applied the day the application opened and notes that most of her fellow interns did the same. A GitHub repo that tracks when tech internship apps go live was a key tool.
* **Insight 2: Networking is non-negotiable.** Cold DM'ing Microsoft employees on LinkedIn for 15-minute coffee chats, then asking each contact to introduce her to another person, created a referral chain she could name-drop in interviews.
* **Insight 3: Your school peers are your real benchmark.** Recruiters compare you to other applicants from your school, not to Stanford students. Finding and talking to alumni who already interned at your target company gives you the most relevant intel.
* **Insight 4: Behavioral interviews are underrated.** Microsoft weighs behavioral questions heavily; using the CARL method (Context, Action, Result, Learning) and being able to speak to every resume bullet point is critical.
* **Insight 5: Communication during the technical interview is a differentiator.** Talking through your thought process out loud, staying positive when stuck, and asking thoughtful closing questions can separate you from candidates with identical solutions.

## Detailed Breakdown / Step-by-Step

**Slide 1 — Introduction:** Ella introduces herself with her Microsoft offer letter, emphasizing her "disadvantages": non-top-20 school, no referral, no projects, and only a sophomore.

**Slide 2 — Apply Early:** The speed of your application matters more than most people think. She applied the day the app opened. She recommends using a GitHub repo that tracks when tech internship applications go live.

**Slide 3 — The Resume:** She used Jake's Resume Template (free, clean, ATS-friendly), copied keywords directly from the job description, used the XYZ bullet method ("Accomplished X by doing Y which resulted in Z"), and listed clubs, leadership roles, and relevant classes to appear well-rounded beyond grades.

**Slide 4 — Networking:** Cold DM'd Microsoft employees on LinkedIn asking for 15-minute coffee chats. Asked two questions: (1) what they look for in candidates, (2) what the culture is like. Asked each person to connect her with someone else to build a chain. Bonus: you can name-drop these conversations in your interview.

**Slide 5 — Talk to People at Your School:** Found students from her own school who had already interned at Microsoft. Asked how they got it, how they prepared, and what made them stand out. Key insight: recruiters compare you to people from your school, not to Stanford students. These peers are your best benchmark.

**Slide 6 — Get Involved on Campus:** Joined an iOS app dev club and actually built things. Became a TA for intro to CS. Held an exec position in her school's SHPE chapter. The point: show you're involved and care, not just taking classes.

**Slide 7 — Technical Interview Prep:** Asked a friend who did the internship what types of questions they ask. Found the same info on Reddit and Glassdoor. Practiced LeetCode questions heavy on data structures and algorithms. Used NeetCode to structure her prep.

**Slide 8 — Behavioral Interview Prep:** Microsoft cares a lot about behavioral questions. Used the CARL method: Context, Action, Result, Learning (the "Learning" part is what sets you apart). Be ready to talk about every single bullet point on your resume. You should never lose an interview because of behavioral questions.

**Slide 9 — The Actual Interview:** Talked through every thought out loud so the interviewer could follow along. Communication can be the difference between you and someone who solved the same problem. Stayed positive even when stuck. Asked 3 questions at the end: (1) What's a project you've really enjoyed lately? (2) What's one thing you'd change about the team? (3) What's made you stay at the company this long?

**Slide 10 — Resources & CTA:** She compiled a list of every resource she used (job boards, interview prep, all of it) into a "PRIME Big Tech Interview Prep Guide" document covering patterns, resources, interview threads, mock interviews, and experience matching.
