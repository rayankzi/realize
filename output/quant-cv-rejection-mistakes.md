# Five Quant CV Mistakes That Get You Instantly Rejected

**Topic/Category:** Career Advice / Quant Finance / Resume Tips
**Primary Goal of the Reel:** To help aspiring quant candidates avoid the most common CV mistakes that lead to immediate rejection by showing what interviewers actually look for.

---

## Executive Summary

This reel breaks down five critical mistakes that cause quant CVs to be rejected — not because the candidate lacks skill, but because the CV fails to communicate how they think. The core message is that quant firms care less about what tools you know and more about how you reason under uncertainty, validate your work, and quantify your impact. The creator provides actionable guidance on shifting from a generic, list-based CV to one that tells a coherent analytical story.

## Core Insights & Takeaways

- **Insight 1:** Tools and languages (Python, C++, SQL) are meaningless without context — tie every skill to a decision, problem, or measurable outcome.
- **Insight 2:** Buzzwords like "AI/ML" without explaining your data, model choice, assumptions, and failure modes are actively harmful — they signal shallow understanding.
- **Insight 3:** Coursework alone doesn't differentiate you — independent projects signal curiosity, self-direction, and the ability to operate without a syllabus.
- **Insight 4:** Quantification is non-negotiable — speedups, error reduction, returns, scale, or robustness metrics prove you measured performance rather than just "built" something.
- **Insight 5:** A coherent narrative beats a long list — clarity about how you approach problems under uncertainty is more valuable than completeness.

## Verbatim Templates & Scripts

The five-point framework itself serves as a direct checklist for CV review:

> **1) Listing tools without demonstrating usage or impact**
> "Python, C++, SQL" is meaningless on its own. Interviewers want to see how you used these tools to reason about a problem. Did you design a simulator? Optimise performance? Handle edge cases? Tools are only impressive when tied to decisions and outcomes.
>
> **2) Buzzwords like AI/ML with no validation**
> "Machine learning" without context is a red flag. What data did you work with? Why that model? How did you evaluate it? If you can't explain assumptions, failure modes, and results, the buzzword hurts more than it helps.
>
> **3) Coursework-only CVs**
> Everyone applying took probability, statistics, and algorithms. Coursework shows exposure — not ownership. Independent projects signal curiosity, self-direction, and the ability to work without a syllabus.
>
> **4) No quantification anywhere**
> If everything is qualitative, nothing stands out. Strong CVs quantify something: speedups, error reduction, returns, scale, or robustness. Numbers show that you measured performance rather than just "built" something.
>
> **5) Overcrowded CV with no narrative**
> A long list of unrelated items hides the signal. A strong CV tells a coherent story about how you approach problems under uncertainty. Clarity beats completeness.
