# Stop Joining Research Labs Without Asking These Questions First

**Topic/Category:** College Advice, Academia, Research
**Primary Goal of the Reel:** To convince undergraduate students to critically evaluate research lab supervisors before committing, rather than just accepting any lab offer.

---

## Executive Summary

This reel warns undergraduates against blindly joining research labs based solely on excitement or the research topic. Drawing from personal experience across multiple labs, the creator argues that the lab environment and supervisor relationship matter far more than the project title itself. They provide three specific questions students should ask potential supervisors before committing, and use the caption to drive engagement by offering a full checklist via DM.

## Core Insights & Takeaways

- **Insight 1:** Most students evaluate labs based on the research topic, but the supervisor determines skill growth, reference letters, and long-term trajectory.
- **Insight 2:** The lab environment shapes your trajectory more than the project title — a great topic under a bad supervisor leads to a worse outcome than a decent topic under a great supervisor.
- **Insight 3:** There are specific, concrete questions you can ask before committing to filter for good supervisors: meeting frequency, realistic deliverables, and outcomes for former undergrads.

## Narrative & Step-by-Step Breakdown

- **Beginning (Hook):** Opens with a direct command — "Stop joining labs without asking these questions" — creating immediate urgency and implying the viewer is making a mistake.
- **Middle (Core Argument):** The creator establishes credibility by mentioning personal experience in multiple research labs, then delivers the key insight: the lab environment matters more than the project title. They then present three targeted questions:
  1. "How often do you meet one-on-one with undergraduates?" — gauges mentorship availability.
  2. "What would a realistic deliverable be for me over the term or the summer?" — tests whether the professor has a structured plan for undergrads.
  3. "Where have your former undergraduates ended up?" — reveals the professor's track record with students like you.
- **End (Conclusion):** Reframes the goal: "Getting into a lab isn't the goal. Getting into the right lab is."
