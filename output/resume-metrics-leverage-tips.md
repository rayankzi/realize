# Why Your Resume Gets Ignored: The Power of Metrics

**Topic/Category:** Career Advice / Resume Writing
**Primary Goal of the Reel:** Teach job seekers — especially new grads targeting fields like investment banking and consulting — why adding quantifiable metrics to resume bullet points is the single most impactful change they can make.

---

## Executive Summary

This reel delivers a sharp, no-nonsense lesson on resume writing: vague descriptions like "I led a product initiative" are worthless to recruiters and hiring managers. The creator argues that adding specific numbers and outcomes — such as "increased activation by 27%" or "shipped a feature used by 3,000 students that improved retention by 20%" — transforms a forgettable resume into one that gets interviews. The caption reinforces this as essential advice for new grads breaking into competitive fields like investment banking and consulting.

## Core Insights & Takeaways

- **Metrics are the single highest-leverage resume improvement.** Without numbers, bullet points lack credibility and give recruiters nothing concrete to evaluate.
- **Vague buzzwords and titles don't differentiate you.** Flaunting your GPA, school name, or generic phrases like "led a product initiative" provides no signal about your actual impact.
- **Specific outcomes help recruiters imagine you in the role.** A line like "shipped a feature used by 3,000 students that improved retention by 20%" lets a hiring manager picture you performing the job they're filling.
- **Metrics improve the interview itself.** When your resume has concrete results, hiring managers ask better, more targeted questions — and you can genuinely speak to your real experiences rather than dancing around vague claims.
- **The formula is: Action + Scope + Measurable Result.** E.g., "Redesigned an onboarding flow → that did X, Y, and Z → increased activation by 27%."

## Narrative & Step-by-Step Breakdown

- **Hook (Opening):** The creator opens with a bold, dismissive statement — "If your resume says 'I led a product initiative,' toss that in the garbage" — immediately creating tension and curiosity.
- **The Problem:** Most resumes flaunt superficial credentials (GPA, school name) and use meaningless buzzwords with no real substance.
- **The Proof Point:** The creator shares a personal example — one specific line on their resume ("increased activation by 27% by redesigning an onboarding flow that did X, Y, and Z") generated more recruiter interest and interviews than anything else.
- **Why It Works:** Numbers give recruiters a concrete signal. A vague "led a product initiative" is useless, but "shipped a feature used by 3,000 students that improved retention by 20%" lets recruiters and hiring managers envision you in the role.
- **The Downstream Effect:** Better resume lines lead to better interview questions, which lead to more authentic conversations about your real experience.
- **Closing Principle:** "Metrics provide credibility. Without metrics, you have no leverage."
